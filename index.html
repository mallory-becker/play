01 — About

The in-between
is the design.

I design the moments between clicks — the easing curves, the hover delays, the tiny lies of timing that make software feel like it has a pulse. Most products treat motion as decoration. I treat it as the load-bearing wall.

I work end-to-end: from the first scribble through React/SwiftUI prototypes that ship. I'm at my best on small teams where I can hold a problem from the ICU through the after-party. I write about easing and have a slightly unreasonable opinion about cubic-bezier(0.34, 1.56, 0.64, 1).

04 — Process

Four moves,
in roughly this order.

01 / Listen

Hear the
real problem.

Most briefs describe the symptom, not the disease. Week one is reading transcripts, sitting in support tickets, and asking the founder what keeps them up. I leave with a one-sentence problem statement — usually different from the one I arrived with.

02 / Sketch

Throw away
the first ten.

Cheap, ugly, on paper. I draw the same screen ten ways before I open Figma. The first three are obvious, four through seven are bad, eight through ten are where the work lives. Then I rebuild the best one with intention.

03 / Prototype

If it can't
move, it's not real.

I prototype in code — React, SwiftUI, sometimes Origami. Static pixels lie. A real prototype tells you instantly if a transition feels right at 60fps in a cold render or if it only worked in the slow-mo screenshot.

04 / Ship

Sit next to
the engineers.

Hand-off documents are where motion design goes to die. I pair with the team during build — adjusting timings live, reviewing PRs, writing the spring config myself. Design isn't done at hand-off. Design is done when it's shipped and nobody's annoyed at it.
