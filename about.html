About — the long version

Hi, I'm Juno.
I move things on screens
for a living.

JP Juno Park · Self portrait, sort of · 2026

I've been designing interfaces for nine years, but I've been animating things since I was seven and convinced PowerPoint to play the cymbal-crash sound effect on every slide change.

I grew up in Seoul, studied at RISD, and now work out of a small studio in Graça, Lisbon. My career has been a slow drift from design that explains toward design that performs. Most product design today is about labels, hierarchies, and decision trees — important, necessary work. What I love is what happens between those decisions: the timing of a state change, the weight of a falling sheet, the way a button absorbs a tap. I think these moments are where products earn or lose your affection.

I work best on small teams where I can hold a problem end-to-end. I prototype in code (React, SwiftUI) because static motion is a lie. I write a newsletter about easing curves that ten people read, three of whom are my mother under different aliases.

    • What I believe — for now

    A small,
    opinionated list.

    01

    Motion isn't decoration.
    It's syntax.

    Where something comes from, how it arrives, how it leaves — these are sentences. Get them wrong and the interface mumbles.

    02

    Defaults are the
    actual product.

    99% of users never change a setting. The default state is the only state. Design for the lazy version of the user, who is also us.

    03

    If you can't
    feel it at 60fps,
    it's not real.

    Slow-motion previews lie. After Effects lies. Origami lies a little. The only honest prototype is the one running on the device, with cold cache, in airplane mode.

    04

    Choose fewer
    things, sharper.

    A portfolio with six projects is better than one with sixteen. A nav with four items is better than one with nine. Less stuff, said better.

    How I like to work

    The fine print, in plain language.

    One thing at a time. I take on two clients at once, max. The first six weeks are full-immersion — I show up to your standups, read your support tickets, learn the names of the engineers' dogs. The next eight weeks are heads-down design and prototyping. Then I hang around for the build.

    Fixed scope, fixed timeline. I quote a project, not an hourly rate. If we agree the scope is "redesign the player," we agree what "done" looks like before we start. Scope creep is just a conversation we haven't had yet — I'd rather have it on day one.

    I prototype in code. Figma is for the team conversation. The thing you actually feel is the thing I build in React or SwiftUI. This usually saves engineering 2–3 sprints because most of the interaction questions are answered before they pick it up.

    Sliding scale for indies and non-profits. If you're bootstrapped, an indie maker, or a non-profit doing work I admire — talk to me. I keep one slot per quarter for projects that pay less than they should.
